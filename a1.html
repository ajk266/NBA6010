x. There are long delays in achieving productivity, and the average productivity does not reflect the productivity increase in specific businesses.

Solow’s argument does not hold because it fails to address the necessary time to appropriately implement information technologies, which defers their impact on productivity. In the long run, overall productivity increases, but not all industries will adapt technology swiftly, effectively and consistently.

b)

y. Productivity increases have been observed decades after the introduction of a technology, and the volatility in technology sector suggests that average productivity does not reflect individual productivity gains.

While technology offers some businesses a competitive advantage, it harms others who cannot appropriately adapt. This turbulence, demonstrated by productivity graphs over the last several decades, is also reflected in the market outcomes for firms falling on either side of this dichotomy.

c)

z. Network effects can reduce value initially, and consequently increase delays in adoption.

At first, network effects inhibit value creation, because in order for their benefit to be realized they need a critical mass of users. If users do not see others on the platform, they may be hesitant to adopt it until it is more widespread. However, over time, as this critical mass is reached, network effects can greatly enhance the value created by information technologies.
