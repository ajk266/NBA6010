b)

What is relationship marketing? Why is it easier to do on the web? Indicate which and briefly explain.
z. It involves tracking customers over time, and it is easier to track the process of buying on the web.
Relationship marketing focuses on tracking customer needs over time, and using the information acquired to flexibly address their needs. Assuming the accuracy of data collected, this is far easier to do and much more inexpensive than one-off marketing. It is easier to do on the web as well, with cookies and similar technologies employed to follow users and better identify their needs, informing marketing efforts.

x. Tracking an existing customer became cheaper than acquiring a new customer because of the database technology.
Relationship marketing is more desirable than mass marketing because it is cheaper and easier and highly useful. Historically, mass marketing offered both cost and ease advantages, making it preferable. However, information technologies like databases and cookies not only made this cheaper, but simpler as well as the Internet evolved and commerce increasingly moved online.

How can online stores exploit the services provided by physical stores? How can physical stores respond? Indicate which and briefly explain.
y. They can utilize the showrooms and sales force of physical stores for physically showing the products, but physical stores can have add on services and charge for them.
Showrooming is a major problem for physical stores, especially as online stores gain traction and market share. Consumers can visit physical stores to learn about products and try them out, but still turn to the Internet to make a purchase. a)

Explain the fundamental tradeoff between inconsistency and information overload in organizational decision-making. How is it related to the centralization-decentralization debate? Indicate which and briefly explain.

y. Decentralized decisions made at the edges of the organization may lead to inconsistency; centralized decisions made at the center may lead to information overload.

Decentralization benefits organizations by limiting information overload, but risks inconsistency of similar decisions are being made in different ways in different parts of the organization, undermining business success. This suggests centralization may address those concerns. However, centralizing decisions runs the risk of information overload as now all data is consolidated, making it difficult to identify information of value due to the volume thereof.

b)

Do network effects tip the balance towards centralization or decentralization according to Braess’ Paradox? Indicate which and briefly explain.

w. Network effects favor centralization.

Braess argues centralization is necessary in network effect cases to prevent suboptimal outcomes resulting from negative network effects. Assuming a lack of centralization, the Braess Paradox demonstrates innovative technologies can trigger worse situations as they do not function in sync with one another and may act “selfishly,” infringing on the optimal outcome.

c)

Braess uses highways to demonstrate his paradox. Do highways have network effects? What kind? Indicate which and briefly explain.

w. Negative but indirect.

Highways have indirect negative network effects, because drivers on them, as Braess discusses, will switch routes continuously. While switching may benefit individual drivers in the short run, the aggregation of switching and traffic redistribution slows down highways overall.

d)

Braess argues that wide spread adoption of new and improved technologies may leave everyone worse off. When? Indicate which and briefly explain with an example of that possibility

x. When there are network effects and the decisions are decentralized.

Decentralized decision-making leads to negative outcomes. In a weapons example, consider nuclear arms. When individual countries hold nuclear weapons, they incentivize others to hold arms so as to secure themselves and establish themselves as nuclear powers as well. However, as more nuclear arms are built, all of human society inches closer to the risk of nuclear holocaust should a war break out.

a)

Explain the basic tradeoff between market share and market size as it relates to technology outsourcing? Indicate which and briefly explain.

y. Outsourcing reduces costs and increases market size; it also eliminates your competitive advantage and reduces your market share.

Outsourcing allows swift and cheap development, reducing complexity and enabling industry standards to emerge. However, this also leads to risk of an eroded competitive advantage and reduced market share as technology becomes commoditized. However, the tradeoff is made because the reduced market share comes with the ability to succeed in a bigger market, albeit a smaller percentage thereof.

b)

Do network effects matter in this tradeoff? How? Indicate which and briefly explain.

x. Network effects favor outsourcing since a larger market size increases the value of the product.

Network effects matter in this case, as they are indicative of an industry standard’s emergence, implying a larger market share. The combination of larger markets and network effects build product value by keeping a high quantity of users on that platform and enhancing market capture, even in the face of the market size-market share tradeoff.

c)

Explain the difference between the strategies of iPhone and Android phones as of 2010. How is it similar to the Apple vs Microsoft battle in the Microcomputer market of the 1980's? How is it different? Indicate which and briefly explain.

z. iPhone tightly controlled hardware design but Android outsourced it; same as Apple vs Microsoft; but unlike Apple of 1980’s iPhone outsourced applications.

The iPhone and Android competition in many ways mirrored that between Apple and Microsoft in the 1980s, with Apple controlling hardware aggressively and Android phones outsourced to a number of developers, most notably Samsung and HTC, in an attempt to grow their network. However, Apple avoided their fatal mistake in the 1980s by outsourcing application development, betting that the vast selection of apps are a key component of the iPhone’s competitive advantage, a strategy that has proven highly successful and enabled the firm to not only grow the market, but also capture market share.

We can safely outsource listings on Yorango, allowing others to post them instead of adding them ourselves. While we risk being seen as a commodity listing site like Craigslist, which could impact market share, we also are able to grow the market and can carve out a niche of a more sizeable space.
